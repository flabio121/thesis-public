\chapter*{ABSTRACT}
Perovskite solar cells can lose performance through mobile-ion motion,
recombination, contact resistance, shunting, and transport-layer changes. These
processes can produce similar current-density--voltage (J--V) curves, so
power-conversion efficiency (PCE) shows that performance changed but not why.
This thesis addresses that diagnostic gap by asking how a physics-based model
can connect measured J--V evolution to hidden electronic, ionic, and degradation
states.

A one-dimensional COMSOL model is developed for the laboratory p-i-n stack,
where p-i-n denotes a p-selective side, intrinsic absorber, and n-selective
side. Drift-diffusion and Poisson electrostatics describe charge transport and
internal fields, while user-defined physics describe mobile-ion redistribution
and bounded degradation states mapped to trap density, ionic charge, series
resistance, shunt resistance, surface recombination, and optical generation.
Aging stress is separated from J--V measurement so slow degradation and fast
voltage scans can be represented on different timescales.

The model is applied to mechanism-isolation cases, parameter sweeps,
accelerated light-plus-heat aging, and light-temperature stress grids.
Comparisons with laboratory J--V data show the main direction of degradation,
including PCE loss, fill-factor reduction, and J--V knee collapse, while
absolute calibration remains incomplete. This work provides a COMSOL-based
framework for mechanism-resolved degradation analysis and future inverse
diagnosis.
