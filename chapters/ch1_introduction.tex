\chapter{Introduction and Scope}

\section{Motivation}

Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have reached efficiencies that justify serious
reliability and manufacturing work, but their degradation remains difficult to
diagnose from terminal electrical measurements alone
\cite{Green_2024,Silverman_2025}. A measured change in J--V shape can result
from mobile-ion redistribution, trap-assisted recombination, transport-layer
electrostatics, surface recombination, shunting, contact resistance, or several
of these mechanisms at the same time. A power-conversion-efficiency (PCE)
trajectory is useful, but it is not by itself a mechanism.

Solar cells convert absorbed photons into electrical current by generating
electron--hole pairs, separating those carriers through internal fields and
selective contacts, and collecting them at electrodes. In a perovskite solar
cell, the absorber is a metal-halide semiconductor with the general
$ABX_3$ perovskite crystal structure. Its composition can be adjusted to tune
band gap, absorption, transport, and stability. Compared with crystalline
silicon, perovskites offer strong absorption in thin films, low-temperature
processing potential, lightweight or flexible form factors, and tandem-cell
opportunities. Their central limitation is operational durability under
illumination, heat, electrical bias, moisture, oxygen, and coupled
interface/electrode reactions.

This thesis develops a COMSOL-based model that connects measurable J--V
behavior to hidden internal mechanisms in a one-dimensional PSC stack. The
model combines semiconductor drift-diffusion, Poisson electrostatics, a
mobile-ion transport equation, damage-state variables, and electrical
resistance mappings. The goal is not to fit one degradation curve with an
empirical trend. The goal is to build a device model whose geometry,
parameters, voltage protocol, internal state variables, and outputs can be
inspected and challenged against physical expectations.

\section{Research Objective}

The objective is to develop a physics-based COMSOL framework for PSC
degradation analysis. The framework links observable J--V behavior to
model-internal quantities such as mobile-ion redistribution, trap growth,
contact degradation, and shunt leakage. It is designed for calibration against
experimental J--V, aging, impedance, transient-current, and optical data, but
the thesis claims are limited to the COMSOL model and the simulation results
available in this repository.

\section{Thesis Contributions}

The first contribution is a COMSOL device model based on a one-dimensional
reduction of the laboratory p-i-n stack. From the illuminated substrate side,
the physical architecture is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{glass}/\mathrm{ITO}/\mathrm{NiO}_x/
\mathrm{MeO\mbox{-}2PACz}/\mathrm{MHP}/
\mathrm{C}_{60}/\mathrm{BCP}/\mathrm{Ag}/
\mathrm{UV\ resin}/\mathrm{glass}.
\end{equation}
The nominal values used in the laboratory description are approximately
30 nm NiO$_x$, 5 nm MeO-2PACz, 25 nm C$_{60}$, 4 nm BCP, 100 nm Ag, and
1.1 mm cover glass. The electronic COMSOL geometry combines the two
hole-selective layers into a 35 nm HTL, combines C$_{60}$ and BCP into a
29 nm ETL, and uses a 450 nm metal-halide perovskite absorber modeled
electronically as Cs$_{0.2}$FA$_{0.8}$PbI$_3$. ITO and Ag are contact
boundaries; glass and UV resin are physical device layers but are not
electronic transport domains in the present model.
The Semiconductor Module solves the coupled electronic transport problem, while
user-defined physics represent mobile-ion redistribution and degradation
states.

The second contribution is a set of mechanism-specific parameter mappings. The
model maps latent degradation states onto COMSOL-accessible quantities such as
trap density, ionic charge density, series resistance, shunt resistance,
surface recombination, and photogeneration. Each mapping has a physical
interpretation, a unit check, and a J--V signature.

The third contribution is a voltage-protocol framework for aging and
hysteresis studies. The model separates the aging bias $V_{\mathrm{stress}}$
from the measurement voltage $V_{\mathrm{app}}(t)$, so slow degradation and
fast J--V scanning can be represented on different timescales. Multi-rate
hysteretic J--V measurements are used as calibration targets for the COMSOL
degradation framework.

The fourth contribution is a qualitative experimental comparison. The COMSOL
baseline and accelerated light-plus-heat degradation case are compared against
laboratory J--V data. The comparison evaluates direction, curve shape, PCE
loss, fill-factor loss, and calibration gaps rather than claiming absolute
lifetime prediction.

The fifth contribution is a first architecture-scoped stress grid for future
surrogate and inverse-model development. The validated one-sun temperature
J--V sweep quantifies how accelerated aging metrics change from 25 to
85~$^\circ$C, while the 6 $\times$ 6 light-temperature profile grid records
spatial summaries of ion, trap, recombination, field, and resistance states.
These data products do not yet constitute a trained surrogate model, but they
define the COMSOL export structure needed for one.

\section{Scope Boundaries}

The main thesis stops at the COMSOL model and results. Machine learning,
surrogate modeling, and latent-state inference are possible uses of the
architecture-stress data set, but trained models are not presented as main-body
claims. The final interpretation is therefore intentionally bounded: the COMSOL
model is a physics-based platform for studying coupled electronic, ionic, and
degradation behavior; the implementation demonstrates qualitative mechanism
separation and produces surrogate-ready state summaries; experimental
calibration remains ongoing.
